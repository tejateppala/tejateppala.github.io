What I do, in simple words:

Current theory predicts that galaxies are infinitely dense at their centers, but they are flatter. I study rainbows of light from galaxies about 100-1000 times less massive than our own Milky Way Galaxy and look for signatures or “fingerprints” of Helium and other elements. I use these fingerprints to figure out if these galaxies are forming stars in intense bursts, and how long these bursts last. This could be the missing piece to complete the puzzle in our understanding of the universe.

My research aims to answer the following questions:
  1. Is star formation in distant low-mass galaxies bursty?
  2. What is the timescale of these star bursts?
  3. How do these bursts contribute to the stellar mass assembly in galaxies?
  4. Do the state-of-the-art models and simulations match the observations?

In current understanding of the universe, there is a fundamental problem of structure formation: the cusp/core problem. There is a discrepancy between our observations of dwarf galaxies and the dark matter haloes predicted by the λCDM model. Pure dark matter simulations predict a central cusp, where the central density of the dark matter halo varies as ρ ˜ r-1. This is inconsistent with the observations of the rotation velocity of neutral hydrogen as a function of radius, which indicate a flatter central density distribution (core) in low-mass galaxies. Some solutions have been proposed, including warm dark matter which challenge our current cosmological framework.

Bursty star formation offers an alternative explanation under the λCDM model. During a star burst episode, gas is driven out very rapidly by supernova and the entire central region expands, bringing the central density down. Over many such episodes, a nearly constant distribution can be formed, consistent with observations. However, there is insufficient evidence of bursty star formation beyond the local universe. This is what my research focuses on — inverstigating the existence of bursty star formation at intermediate redshifts: 0.4 ≤ z ≤ 0.6 (7-9 Billion years after Big Bang).

These star bursts occur on timescales of a few to tens of millions of years; therefore although we may find a galaxy in midst of a star burst, we don't have any idea how long a burst lasts. I use spectra from ground-based and space-based telescopes to look for signatures of these star bursts and try to constrain their timescales through modeling. Using these constarints, the current model can be improved to match the observations.
